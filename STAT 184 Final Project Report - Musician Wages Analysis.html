Introduction

Growing up I was surrounded by music. My father played piano nearly every day, and my sister the clarinet. In 4th grade, I picked up the clarinet and joined the school band. Since then, I have been in countless bands, orchestras, and jazz bands, playing clarinet, saxophone, and flute. When I started this project, I was excited to find a dataset created by the New Music Organizing Caucus, which gathers information about gigs of musicians. I then compare this dataset to information gathered from the Bureau of Labor Statistics on musicians and singers. This allows me to validate the data gathered from the Caucus, and analyze the distribution of musicians across the country, as well as the nature of the gigs they are getting.

Research Question

How does the distribution of professional musicians and their wage vary across the United States?

Location Quotient (Musician density)

In order to answer our research question, we need a statistic that describes the concentration of musicians and singers in each area/state. Luckily for us, within the BLS dataset, there is a feature called the “Location Quotient”. The BLS defines location quotient as:

“Location quotients compare the concentration of an industry within a specific area to the concentration of that industry nationwide”

— https://www.bls.gov/cew/about-data/location-quotients-explained.htm

By analyzing the distribution of location quotient, we can get a good understanding of where there a high concentration of musicians throughout the country.

Let’s consider the distribution of this feature.

avgLocQuot <- mean(BLS$`Location Quotient`, na.rm = TRUE)

BLS %>%
  ggplot(aes(x = `Location Quotient`)) +
  geom_histogram(aes(x = `Location Quotient`)) +
  geom_vline(aes(xintercept = avgLocQuot), linetype = "dashed", color = "blue") +
  labs(title = "Distribution of Location Quotient of musicians and singers\n throughout the United States")

The distribution is skewed right with an average of 1.67. (It makes sense that the average is a little above 1, since the data is skewed right, and that the data collection is not perfect.) Let’s look at the outliers, as they would be areas with especially high concentration of musicians and singers. A common practice for classifying outliers is any value more than the 3rd quartile + 1.5 x IQR can be considered an outlier.

The second dataset holds some interesting information regarding music gigs. In particular, what organization paid for the gigs, and how long they were. Let’s take a look at the length of the gigs, first.

Conclusion

The density of musicians throughout the United States tends be heavier in the northeast of the country, and less in the South and western portions. The hourly wage of musicians also tends to follow this trend, with higher pay in the northeast. However, when comparing the trend of musician population density (location quotient) versus average hourly wage, there does not seem to be a relationship. The explanation may be that hourly wage is more dependent on geographic area, rather than a more local perspective in relation to how many musicians are, say, in New York City vs the suburbs. This could be a topic for future research.
